Arabica Coffee

Across the globe, certain types of coffee have gained immense popularity due to their flavor profiles, cultural significance, and widespread consumption. Arabica coffee (Coffea arabica) is the most consumed coffee variety, accounting for around 60-70% of global production. Known for its smooth, delicate flavor and low acidity, it’s primarily grown in high-altitude regions of Latin America, Ethiopia, and Southeast Asia. Arabica coffee is favored for its nuanced taste and is commonly found in specialty coffee shops worldwide.

Arabica beans are commonly used for specialty coffees and are valued for their diversity in taste profiles, depending on the region, altitude, and processing methods.

Arabica coffee plants thrive in higher altitudes, typically between 600 and 2,200 meters above sea level. The ideal growing conditions for Arabica include tropical climates with consistent rainfall and moderate temperatures (between 15-24°C or 60-75°F).

Cappuccino

Cappuccino is one of the most popular and iconic coffee drinks worldwide, with origins in Italy. It is traditionally made by combining equal parts of espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam. This balance creates a rich, creamy texture with a strong coffee flavor. The name "cappuccino" is derived from the Capuchin monks, whose brown robes are said to resemble the drink’s color when the milk is added to the espresso.

While it has a morning association in Europe, cappuccino is now consumed throughout the day in many countries, especially in coffee shops in North America and beyond.

Cappuccino is typically served in a smaller cup, usually between 150-180 ml, which emphasizes the bold flavor of the espresso. The foam on top, which is about a third of the drink, acts as an insulator, keeping the coffee warm while also giving it a smooth mouthfeel.
